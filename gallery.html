The Lives and Works of the Masters

Katsushika Hokusai

Katsushika Hokusai, born in 1760 in Edo (present-day Tokyo), Japan, was an influential and prolific artist of the Edo period renowned for his ukiyo-e woodblock prints. Perhaps his most famous work, "The Great Wave off Kanagawa," is part of the "Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji" series, celebrated globally for its dynamic composition and striking use of color. His innovative approaches and mastery of ukiyo-e greatly influenced not only Japanese art but also significantly impacted Western Impressionism. Hokusai's dedication to his craft persisted throughout his life, famously expressing a desire to continue improving his work well into old age.

Utagawa Hiroshige

Utagawa Hiroshige, born in 1797 in Edo (now Tokyo), Japan, is considered the last master of ukiyo-e, and is renowned for his landscapes and depictions of birds and flowers. His most famous series, "The Fifty-three Stations of the Tōkaidō," showcases scenes from the Tōkaidō road, the highway connecting Edo to Kyoto, capturing the beauty of various landscapes through different seasons and times of day. Hiroshige's use of color and perspective brought a new, lyrical dimension to the traditional ukiyo-e style, influencing the development of the genre. His work had a profound impact not only in Japan but also on Western artists of the Impressionist and Post-Impressionist movements, such as Van Gogh and Monet, who admired and drew inspiration from his prints. Hiroshige continued to produce art until his death in 1858, leaving behind a legacy that cemented his status as one of the great masters of Japanese woodblock printing.
